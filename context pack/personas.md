## Persona 1: Marcus — the Project Manager

**Role:** Project Manager at a 200-person architecture and construction firm. Manages 4–6 active projects simultaneously. Has been in the role 6 years. Not technical, but highly process-oriented.

**JTBD:** When a project moves from design completion into the build phase, I want to know that the field team has the right, current drawings without me having to manually track down confirmations — so I can focus on the next problem instead of babysitting file distribution.

**Biggest frustration with current product:** The publish-to-client step is confusing and I'm never sure it actually worked. I've sent drawings to the field that were the wrong version twice this year because the system showed "published" but the client portal didn't update correctly. I don't trust the confirmation.

**What success looks like for them:** Marcus opens the project dashboard in the morning and can see — without clicking into anything — which drawings are live with the field, which approvals are pending, and whether anything changed overnight. He gets one daily digest instead of 40 individual notifications. He hasn't had a version mix-up in three months.

**How they discover problems:** Marcus finds out something is broken when a field team lead calls him. He's almost never the first person to notice a Terraloom issue — he finds out through downstream consequences (wrong drawing on site, missed deadline, confused subcontractor).

**Biggest risk to retention:** If Marcus has one more version mix-up that causes a field delay, his firm's PM director will evaluate alternatives. He's already been asked to "look into other options" once.

## Persona 2: Priya — the Architect / Design Lead

**Role:** Senior Architect and informal design team lead at a 90-person architecture firm. Responsible for producing and publishing drawing sets. Uses Terraloom primarily as a file publishing tool, not a project management tool.

**JTBD:** When I finalize a drawing revision, I want to publish it to the construction team and the client in one action — so I can move on to the next design task without worrying about whether the right people got the right version.

**Biggest frustration with current product:** The publish workflow has too many steps and I'm not sure what each one does. I don't know the difference between "publish to project" and "publish to client." I've accidentally published an internal draft to the client-facing view twice. The permissions model is opaque — I don't know who can see what until someone tells me they can't see something.

**What success looks like for them:** Priya finishes a drawing set, clicks publish, selects her recipients (internal team, construction lead, client), and gets a confirmation that tells her exactly who received it and what they can see. She never has to follow up to ask "did you get the drawings?" That question goes away.

**How they discover problems:** Priya discovers problems when someone on the construction team calls or emails asking for a file she thought she already sent. Or when a client asks for something she published two weeks ago and can't find it in the portal. Her awareness of Terraloom problems is entirely reactive.

**Biggest risk to retention:** Priya's firm is small enough that if the principal architect decides Terraloom is too complicated, they'll switch to Dropbox and a shared spreadsheet. She's the internal advocate for Terraloom — if she stops advocating, the renewal is at risk.

## Persona 3: Devon — the Field Superintendent

**Role:** Construction Superintendent at a 300-person general contractor. Manages the day-to-day build on 1–2 active job sites. Spends most of the day on-site, not at a desk. Uses a tablet and phone. Technically capable but has zero patience for tools that don't work immediately.

**JTBD:** When I need to check a structural detail or confirm a spec on-site, I want to pull up the current approved drawing on my phone in under 30 seconds — so I can answer my subcontractor's question and keep the work moving without walking back to the trailer.

**Biggest frustration with current product:** The mobile app doesn't have the drawings I need half the time. Either they weren't published to mobile or I can't find them in the folder structure. I've given up on Terraloom on-site and email PDFs to myself instead. The notification emails I get from Terraloom are useless — I get 20 a day and none of them tell me what I actually need to know, which is "a drawing you're using was updated."

**What success looks like for them:** Devon gets one notification per week that says "3 drawings on your active projects were updated — here's what changed." He opens the mobile app, the current drawing is the first thing he sees for each active project, and it loads in under 5 seconds on a cell connection. He hasn't emailed a PDF to himself in two months.

**How they discover problems:** Devon discovers Terraloom problems when something goes wrong on-site — a subcontractor builds to an old spec, a detail doesn't match what the drawings show, a material order is wrong. By the time he knows there's a version problem, the damage is done. He doesn't file support tickets; he just stops using the tool that failed him.

**Biggest risk to retention:** Devon is not the buyer — his firm's project director is. But if Devon stops using Terraloom on-site and the field team's active seat ratio drops, the PM director will notice at renewal and question the value. Devon's disengagement is a leading indicator of churn, not the churn itself.
